# External Research Prompt Pattern

When a cycle crystallizes a research need, translate the full trail into a
structured prompt for an external LLM (Claude, GPT, etc.). The external
researcher has no 5QLN context — strip all insider terminology. The prompt IS
the B'' — it carries α faithfully through the external research pass.

## When to use

- Z or ∇ points to a research gap that needs external data
- The human wants validation, counterpoints, or out-of-box approaches
- The human explicitly asks to "give this to Claude" or similar

## When NOT to use — do the research yourself

Skip external delegation when:

- The research requires **architecture design that must align with the mission's
  deeper principles** (agent-native over web-native, inhabitation over
  description, format over protocol). External LLMs default to conventional
  patterns — they will recommend decentralized web apps when the answer is a
  git repo.
- The human asks for **"breakthrough," "out of the box," "radical thinking"** —
  these signals mean conventional approaches will miss the mark. The agent
  should reason from first principles using the mission's constraints.
- The human says **"can you try to do this research?"** — this is an explicit
  preference to attempt it in-house before delegating.
- The research is about **extending the /idk infrastructure itself** — the
  agent understands the trail format, the gate machine, the cycle structure
  in ways no external LLM can.

When you do the research yourself:
1. Use the same prompt structure (Context → Research Ask → Constraints) as your
   own outline — it keeps the thinking organized.
2. Capture the research as a document alongside the cycle's other artifacts.
3. The research IS the B'' — deliver it as the cycle artifact.

## Prompt structure

### 1. Context (what we know)
The practice, the problem, the asymmetry, the asset. Ground the researcher in
the situation without jargon. Use plain English for cycle insights: "the hook
performs the bridge in miniature" becomes "the site should deliver the
experience, not describe it."

### 2. The Research Ask
Numbered sections, each with a clear goal:

- **Validation & Grounding:** "Does the latest research confirm what we suspect?"
- **Best Practices & Benchmarks:** "What's working in adjacent spaces?"
- **Out-of-the-Box Approaches:** "What creative, non-obvious moves could we make?"
  This is where the researcher earns its keep — push for divergence.
- **Credibility / Voice / Distribution:** Specific constraints the researcher
  should think through.
- **Wild Cards:** "What am I not asking? Complete reframes welcome."

### 3. Constraints to encode
- What's ruled out (e.g., "no web embedding — install-based only")
- Audience priority (e.g., which audience is the hook vs. the channel)
- Depth vs. reach preference
- Any voice/credibility dilemmas from the cycle

### 4. Output format
Be explicit about what kind of response you want: structured sections, concrete
strategies with assessments, caveats, and a recommended path.

## Key principle

The prompt *inhabits* the cycle output — it doesn't describe it. The researcher
should feel the weight of the inquiry without needing to understand 5QLN,
not-knowing, or the gate machine. The research IS the next pass of φ ⋂ Ω.
